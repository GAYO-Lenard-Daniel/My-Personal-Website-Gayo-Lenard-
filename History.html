About Me


Traditional American and British folk music in particular influenced the folk revival from the 30s - 40s artists such as Woody Guthrie and Pete Seeger. Woody Guthrie in particular was enormously influential to the budding 20th-century folk revival and his significant contributions to the spread and preservation of traditional folk music. He was taught by both his parents folk songs that they had learned as well in his early life which he carried throughout playing and singing in the hobo and migrant camps later in his life. And during World War II, wrote more anti-fascist songs and became his most active after marrying his second wife with songs such as "Don't You Push Me Down," "Ship In The Sky", and "Howdi Doo”.

His influence stretched across artists such as Phil Ochs, Bruce Springsteen, Bob Dylan, and so many more. Folk music boomed during the 50s - 60s with artists such as the aforementioned artists along with Joan Baez, Buffy Sainte-Marie, and Arlo Guthrie, who is Woodie Guthrie’s son, who, like Woody Guthrie, also instilled political messaging in their songwriting with the convergence of the civil rights movement, following his footsteps. The folk revival typically reflects the struggles of poverty and disempowerment of the working class and also expresses anger and disdain towards the government and other issues such as war.

Feminist-inspired cultural change was also expressed in folk music from artists such as Joan Baez, Judy Collins, and Buffy Sainte-Marie, in which they resisted traditional gender roles and advocated for the awareness of women-specific issues. Much of the folk music of that era also advocated for racial equality. This was more evident when Joan Baez, Odetta, Bob Dylan, Peter, Paul & Mary, and Marian Anderson broadcasted their performance in a protest for equal rights back in 1963, which was a pivotal event in the Civil Rights Movement. It wasn’t all political though. Themes of romance, heartbreak, and longing were also common at that time with songs such as “Girl From the North Country” by Bob Dylan, “A Case of You” by Joni Mitchell, and “Suzanne” by Leonard Cohen.
